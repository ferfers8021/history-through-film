# Lesson Plan: *Gladiator* (2000)
**Historical Period:** Late Roman Empire (c. 180–192 CE)  
**Film Director:** Ridley Scott  
**Target Audience:** High school or undergraduate history students  
**Suggested Time:** 2–3 class periods (film viewing outside of class, or in-class excerpts)

---

## 1. Historical Context

### The Roman Empire in the Late 2nd Century CE
- **Marcus Aurelius** (r. 161–180 CE): The last of the “Five Good Emperors,” a Stoic philosopher who spent much of his reign fighting Germanic tribes along the Danube frontier. His death in 180 CE marked the end of the *Pax Romana*.
- **Commodus** (r. 180–192 CE): Marcus Aurelius’s son, who succeeded him. Historical accounts (Cassius Dio, Herodian) depict Commodus as erratic, megalomaniacal, and unfit to rule. He preferred gladiatorial spectacle to governance and was eventually assassinated.
- **The Roman Army:** A professional, disciplined force organized into legions and auxiliary units. Soldiers served 25‑year terms and were stationed across the empire’s frontiers.
- **Gladiatorial Games (*munera*):** Originally funeral rites, by the Imperial period they had become state‑sponsored entertainment. Gladiators were mostly slaves, prisoners of war, or condemned criminals, though some free men voluntarily fought for fame and money. Successful gladiators could gain popularity and, rarely, freedom.
- **Political Structure:** The Principate (early Empire) maintained the façade of the Republic while concentrating power in the hands of the emperor. The Praetorian Guard, the emperor’s personal military unit, often played a king‑making role.

### Key Historical Figures
- **Marcus Aurelius** – Emperor, Stoic philosopher, author of *Meditations*.
- **Commodus** – His son and successor, portrayed as a tyrant.
- **Lucilla** – Commodus’s older sister, who was involved in a failed conspiracy against him.
- **Gaius Maximus Meridius** – Fictional protagonist; no historical counterpart.

## 2. Film’s Portrayal

### Narrative Summary
*Gladiator* follows the fictional general Maximus Decimus Meridius, who is betrayed by Commodus after Marcus Aurelius’s death. Enslaved as a gladiator, Maximus rises through the arena to confront Commodus in Rome, ultimately avenging his family and restoring the Senate’s dignity.

### Cinematic and Thematic Choices
- **Visual Style:** Sweeping landscapes, gritty battle scenes, and grandiose arena spectacles create an epic feel.
- **Character Archetypes:** Maximus embodies the virtuous, self‑sacrificing hero; Commodus represents decadent, narcissistic tyranny; Lucilla is caught between loyalty to her brother and the good of Rome.
- **Political Message:** The film champions republican ideals (the Senate, the restoration of the Republic) over imperial autocracy, though historically the Senate had lost most of its real power by this period.
- **Portrayal of Gladiatorial Combat:** Emphasizes brutality and spectacle, while also humanizing gladiators as individuals with dreams and loyalties.

### Strengths of the Portrayal
- Accurately captures the scale and opulence of the Roman Empire.
- Effectively conveys the tension between military duty and personal loyalty.
- Highlights the role of spectacle in maintaining imperial power (bread and circuses).
- Provides a visceral sense of the chaos and violence of ancient combat.

## 3. Key Inaccuracies

| Inaccuracy | Historical Reality | Why the Film Might Have Taken This Liberty |
|------------|-------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| **Marcus Aurelius is murdered by Commodus** | Marcus Aurelius died of natural causes (likely plague or illness) while on campaign along the Danube. | Creates a more dramatic villain and a clear motive for Maximus’s revenge. |
| **Marcus Aurelius wants to restore the Republic** | There is no evidence that Marcus Aurelius planned to abolish the Principate. The idea of “giving power back to the people” is a modern democratic ideal projected onto the past. | Makes the conflict between Maximus and Commodus ideologically charged and gives the hero a noble cause. |
| **Commodus fights in the arena as a gladiator** | Commodus did perform in the arena, but as a *venator* (hunter of beasts) and in staged, non‑lethal combats. He never fought to the death against skilled gladiators. | Amplifies Commodus’s megalomania and sets up the final duel with Maximus. |
| **The Germanian Wars are depicted as a single, decisive battle** | The Marcomannic Wars were a series of protracted campaigns, not a single climactic engagement. | Simplifies the military narrative for cinematic pacing. |
| **Maximus is a Spanish general who becomes a gladiator** | No historical record of a general turned gladiator. Gladiators were typically from the lower classes or prisoners. | Creates an underdog story and allows the audience to see the arena from an outsider’s perspective. |
| **The Colosseum is shown as completed in 180 CE** | The Colosseum (Flavian Amphitheatre) was inaugurated in 80 CE, so it was indeed standing. However, the film’s depiction of its interior (elevator systems, trap doors) exaggerates its technological sophistication for the time. | Enhances visual spectacle and the sense of imperial engineering. |
| **Lucilla’s romantic involvement with Maximus** | Lucilla was married to Lucius Verus (co‑emperor with Marcus Aurelius) and later to Claudius Pompeianus. There is no evidence of an affair with a general like Maximus. | Adds a personal, emotional layer to the political plot. |

## 4. Discussion Questions

1. **Historical vs. Dramatic Truth:** *Gladiator* takes many liberties with historical facts. Do you think these changes make the film more or less effective as a teaching tool about the Roman Empire? Why?
2. **Character Motivation:** Compare Commodus as portrayed in the film with historical accounts. What aspects of his personality does the film emphasize, and what might it omit? How does this shape our view of imperial power?
3. **Spectacle and Power:** The film repeatedly shows crowds cheering in the arena. How did gladiatorial games serve political purposes in ancient Rome? Can you draw parallels to modern‑day spectacles (sports, media events) used by governments or leaders?
4. **Gender Roles:** Examine the roles of Lucilla and Maximus’s wife (seen only in flashbacks). What do these portrayals suggest about women’s agency in the Roman world? How does the film handle their characters compared to historical realities?
5. **Republic vs. Empire:** Marcus Aurelius and Maximus both speak of “restoring the Republic.” Why might the filmmakers have chosen to inject this democratic ideal into a story set in the late 2nd century? What does this tell us about modern perspectives on ancient governance?
6. **Violence and Heroism:** The film glorifies Maximus’s combat skills and his journey from general to gladiator. Does the portrayal of violence serve a historical purpose, or does it primarily entertain? How might ancient Romans have viewed such a hero?
7. **Legacy of Rome:** *Gladiator* ends with the hope of a “better Rome.” How does the film use the past to comment on contemporary ideas of justice, leadership, and civic duty?

## 6. Classroom Activities

1. **“Fact vs. Fiction” Chart:** Have students create a two‑column chart listing key events/characters in the film and the corresponding historical reality.
2. **Stoicism Debate:** Read excerpts from Marcus Aurelius’s *Meditations* and discuss how Stoic philosophy contrasts with Commodus’s hedonism. How might a Stoic emperor have ruled differently?
3. **Gladiator Diaries:** Ask students to write a journal entry from the perspective of a gladiator (slave, prisoner, or volunteer) describing a day in the *ludus* (gladiator school) and their feelings about fighting in the arena.
4. **Senate Simulation:** Divide the class into “senators,” “praetorians,” and “emperors” to debate a fictional crisis (e.g., succession after Marcus Aurelius). This highlights the power dynamics of the late Principate.
5. **Film Review Assignment:** Write a critical review of *Gladiator* that evaluates its historical accuracy, its strengths as a drama, and its value for learning about ancient Rome.
